I write a lot of what I publish with help from AI now. This post included, if I'm honest. Which makes the whole thing a bit of a hall of mirrors, but stay with me.

There's an old question you've probably heard. If a tree falls in a forest and nobody's around to hear it, does it make a sound?

I've been turning over a different version of it. If a thought is mine, but the words are assembled by a machine, is it still my thought? Does the writing count?

### So who actually wrote it?

Here's what happens when I draft with AI. I have the idea. The itch, the argument, the thing I want to say. That part is entirely me. It came from a walk, or a bad meeting, or something my kid said. Then the machine takes it and lays down a run of words that tend to follow one another, arranged into a rough likeness of what I meant.

It's a good likeness. Sometimes better phrased than I'd manage on my own. But it didn't have the thought. It filled in around it.

So the question isn't really "did AI write this". It's "where does the value live". And I think the value lives in the thought, not the typing.

### Does that let me off the hook?

Not entirely. Because a reader can't see the thought. They see the words. And if the words are frictionless and polished and competent, they've no way of knowing whether there's a person underneath or just a very confident autocomplete.

That's the actual problem with AI writing. Not that it's fake. That it removes the tells. The clumsy phrase that showed someone cared. The odd word choice only one person would reach for.

### So should you dismiss it?

You've got two options as a reader. Skim the surface, clock that it reads a bit smooth, and write it off. Or go the other way and dig for the thought underneath, then judge that instead.

Most people pick the first one. Not because they're lazy. Because there's too much of everything and attention is the one thing nobody's managed to manufacture more of. Infinite words, a finite number of seconds to care about them.

Which, honestly, is the bit that worries me. Not that machines can write. That we might stop reading properly because we assume nothing's worth the dig anymore.

### Where I've landed

The tree makes a sound. Physics doesn't care who's listening. And a thought is a thought whether I typed every word or a machine helped me lay them down.

But making a sound isn't the same as being heard. That part still needs a person on the other end willing to do the work of listening. I can automate the writing. I can't automate you caring.

So I'll keep using the machine to build the words. The thoughts are still mine. The only open question is whether anyone's still around to hear them fall.
